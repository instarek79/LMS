Lighting and Shading

 

  • The brightness of a pixel in the image is a function of the brightness of the surface patch in the scene that projects to the pixel.
  • People can usually tell whether changes of brightness are caused by changes in reflection or by shading

 

 MODELLING PIXEL BRIGHTNESS

  • Three major phenomena determine the brightness of a pixel:
    • Camera response: Modern cameras respond linearly to middling intensities of light but have pronounced nonlinearities for darker and brighter
       illumination.
    • Surface reflection:  Darker surfaces reflect less light, and lighter surfaces reflect more
      • Diffuse reflection: Incident light is (reflected) spread evenly over the whole hemisphere of outgoing (e.g. rough surface)
      • Specular reflection: reflected light is concentrated in a single direction (e.g. flat mirror/ flat surface)

    • Illumination: The amount of light a patch receives depends on the overall intensity of the light, and on the geometry.

       

       

Reflection at Surfaces

  •  Most surfaces reflect light by a process of diffuse reflection.
  • albedo is a parameter required to describe a surface make a diffuse reflection
  • albedo(diffuse reflectance (DHR) ) parameter lies in the range 0.05 – 0.90 (0 is black surface, 1 is white surface)

Reflection Mechanisms

  • surface reflection
    • Specular reflection
    • glossy appearance
    • smooth surfaces (e.g. mirror, glass)
  • body reflection
    • diffuse reflection
    • matte appearance
    • Non-homogeneous medium (e.g. clay, paper)

Image intensity =body reflection + surface reflection

Illumination Sources and Their Effects

  • The main source of illumination outdoors is the sun
  • The sun is modeled as a distant point light source
  • The amount of light collected by a surface patch in this model is proportional to the cosine of the angle θ between the illumination direction and the normal (Lambert’s cosine law) [ I = ρI0 cos θ  ] where I is the intensity of the light source, θ is the angle between the light source direction and the surface normal, and ρ is the diffuse albedo

Radiance

  • All this suggests that the light transferred from source to receiver should be measured as Radiant power per unit foreshortened area per unit solid angle
  • Units:  watts per square meter per steradian  (wm-2sr-1)
  • In a vacuum, radiance leaving p in the direction of q is the same as radiance arriving at q from p
  • Radiance is constant along straight line
 
Irradiance
  • How much light is arriving at a surface? Sensible unit is Irradiance
  • Incident power per unit area not foreshortened
  • This is a function of incoming angle.  A surface experiencing radiance L(x,q,f) coming in from dw experiences irradiance
  • Total power arriving at the surface is given by adding irradiance over all incoming angles

The Lambertian and Specular Model (Body reflection)

  • The Bidirectional Reflectance Distribution Function (BRDF) is the ratio of the radiance in the outgoing direction to the incident irradiance. 
    • It has two angles zenith angle and Azimuth angle
    • the BRDF is the ratio between L and E, it must greater than zero, it has a commutative property of source and camera(Helmholtz reciprocity) 
    • it 4-dimension functions, it isotropic and anisotropic 
  • In a Lambertian surface, BRDF is independent of angle, too.

 

Lambert’s wall

  • In Lambert’s wall, we assume that the sky is overcast, that the horizontal plane is uniform, and that the vertical wall is black 
  • Every point sees the same input hemisphere, and as a result each point must be the same
  • Principle:  two sources that look the same to a receiver must have the same effect on the receiver.
  • Reason: what else can a receiver know about a source but what appears on its input hemisphere? (ditto, swapping receiver and source)
  • Principle:  two receivers that look the same to a source must receive the same amount of energy.
  • Crucial consequence: a big source (resp. receiver), viewed at a glancing angle, must produce (resp. experience) the same effect as a small source (resp. receiver) viewed frontally.
  • “look the same” means produce the same input hemisphere (or output hemisphere)

 

Area Sources

  • An area source is an area that radiates light
  • The intensity at a surface patch illuminated by an area source,  can break the source up into infinitesimal source elements, then sum effects from each element. 
  • Patches will be dark, that lie in the umbra 
  • Patches may be quite bright (if they see most of the elements), or that lie in the penumbra
