11.6 Inferential Statistics

Welcome every one  in this lesson we'll explore what inferential statistics is, its key terms, and provide examples to help you understand each concept. Inferential statistics allows us to make predictions or inferences about a population based on a sample of data. It's a powerful tool used in various fields, including science, business, and social sciences.

 What is Inferential Statistics?

Inferential statistics involves using data from a sample to draw conclusions about a larger population. Unlike descriptive statistics, which only describes the sample data, inferential statistics aims to make generalizations and predictions.

Key Terms in Inferential Statistics]

Let's dive into the main terms used in inferential statistics:

  1. Population

    • Definition: The entire group that you want to draw conclusions about.

    • Example: If you're studying the average height of adult men in the U.S., the population would be all adult men in the U.S.

  2. Sample

    • Definition: A subset of the population that is used to collect data and make inferences.

    • Example: A group of 1,000 adult men selected randomly from different states in the U.S.

  3. Parameter

    • Definition: A numerical value that describes a characteristic of the population.

    • Example: The average height of all adult men in the U.S. is a parameter.

  4. Statistic

    • Definition: A numerical value that describes a characteristic of the sample.

    • Example: The average height of the 1,000 sampled adult men.

  5. Hypothesis Testing

    • Definition: A method used to test an assumption or claim about a population parameter.

    • Example: Testing if the average height of adult men in the U.S. is different from 5 feet 10 inches.

  6. Null Hypothesis (H0)

    • Definition: A statement that there is no effect or no difference, and it serves as the starting point for hypothesis testing.

    • Example: The null hypothesis might state that the average height of adult men in the U.S. is 5 feet 10 inches.

  7. Alternative Hypothesis (H1)

    • Definition: A statement that contradicts the null hypothesis, indicating there is an effect or a difference.

    • Example: The alternative hypothesis might state that the average height of adult men in the U.S. is not 5 feet 10 inches.

  8. P-value

    • Definition: The probability of obtaining test results at least as extreme as the observed results, assuming the null hypothesis is true.

    • Example: A p-value of 0.03 means there is a 3% chance of observing the data if the null hypothesis is true.

  9. Confidence Interval

    • Definition: A range of values that is likely to contain the population parameter with a certain level of confidence.

    • Example: A 95% confidence interval for the average height might be 5 feet 9 inches to 5 feet 11 inches.

  10. Significance Level (alpha)

    • Definition: A threshold for deciding when to reject the null hypothesis, commonly set at 0.05.

    • Example: If the p-value is less than 0.05, you reject the null hypothesis.

Example of Inferential Statistics

Let's go through an example to tie everything together. Imagine you're a researcher studying a new drug's effect on blood pressure. You want to know if the drug significantly lowers blood pressure in the population.

  1. Define the Population: All patients with high blood pressure.

  2. Select a Sample: 100 patients with high blood pressure.

  3. Calculate the Statistic: The average drop in blood pressure in the sample is 10 mmHg.

  4. Formulate Hypotheses:

    • Null Hypothesis (H0): The drug has no effect on blood pressure (mean drop = 0 mmHg).

    • Alternative Hypothesis (H1): The drug lowers blood pressure (mean drop > 0 mmHg).

  5. Conduct Hypothesis Testing: Use a statistical test to calculate the p-value.

  6. Determine the P-value: Suppose the p-value is 0.02.

  7. Compare with Significance Level: Since 0.02 < 0.05, you reject the null hypothesis.

  8. Draw Conclusion: There is significant evidence to suggest the drug lowers blood pressure.

11.6.2  Understanding the Basics of Statistical Inference

Statistical inference is the process of making conclusions about a population based on a sample. It involves estimating population parameters and testing hypotheses. There are two main types of statistical inference: estimation and hypothesis testing.

Example:

Suppose we want to estimate the average height of all adults in a city.

Python Code:

import numpy as np

# Generate sample data
np.random.seed(42)
sample_heights = np.random.normal(loc=170, scale=10, size=100)

# Estimate population mean
sample_mean = np.mean(sample_heights)
sample_std = np.std(sample_heights, ddof=1)
confidence_interval = (sample_mean - 1.96 * sample_std / np.sqrt(len(sample_heights)),
                       sample_mean + 1.96 * sample_std / np.sqrt(len(sample_heights)))

print(f"Sample Mean: {sample_mean}")
print(f"95% Confidence Interval: {confidence_interval}")

Here, we’ve generated a sample of 100 heights from a normal distribution and calculated the sample mean and 95% confidence interval.

    'Grade': np.random.choice(['A', 'B', 'C'], 100),

    'Score': np.random.normal(loc=75, scale=10, size=100)

strata = df.groupby('Grade', group_keys=False).apply(lambda x: x.sample(min(len(x), 10)))

Which sampling method ensures that subgroups within a population are adequately represented?

  • C) Stratified sampling

  • D) Cluster sampling

 The correct answer is C) Stratified sampling.

11.6.3 Main Pitfalls of Sampling

While sampling is essential, it’s crucial to be aware of common pitfalls. These include bias, non-representative samples, and small sample sizes.

Example:

Suppose we’re conducting an online survey about internet usage, but only people with internet access can respond. This introduces selection bias.

Python Code:

# Example of potential sampling bias

internet_users = np.random.choice(['Yes', 'No'], 100, p=[0.8, 0.2])

survey_respondents = internet_users[internet_users == 'Yes']



print(f"Survey Respondents: {len(survey_respondents)} out of 100")

print(f"Bias: {100 - len(survey_respondents)} people without internet access were not surveyed.")

This code demonstrates how selection bias can occur when the sample isn’t representative of the entire population.

 What is a major consequence of selection bias in sampling?

  • A) Increased sample size

  • B) Non-representative sample

  • C) Accurate population parameter estimates

  • D) None of the above

The correct answer is B) Non-representative sample. Selection bias leads to samples that don’t accurately represent the population.

11.6.5 Designing Experiments

Host: Designing experiments involves planning how to collect data to answer specific research questions. Key elements include control groups, randomization, and replication.

Example:

Let’s design an experiment to test the effect of a new drug.

Python Code:

# Generate sample data

np.random.seed(42)

group = np.random.choice(['Control', 'Treatment'], 100)

outcome = np.where(group == 'Treatment', np.random.normal(loc=5, scale=1.5, size=100), np.random.normal(loc=3, scale=1.5, size=100))



# Create DataFrame

experiment_data = pd.DataFrame({'Group': group, 'Outcome': outcome})



# Summary statistics

print(experiment_data.groupby('Group').mean())

This code simulates an experiment with control and treatment groups and calculates the mean outcome for each group.

What is the purpose of a control group in an experiment?

  • A) To provide a baseline for comparison

  • B) To receive the experimental treatment

  • C) To introduce bias

  • D) None of the above

 The correct answer is A) To provide a baseline for comparison. Control groups help us understand the effect of the treatment.

11.6.6 Evaluating the Effectiveness of Designed Experiments

Evaluating experiments involves analyzing the results to determine if the observed effects are statistically significant and practically meaningful.

Example:

Continuing with our drug experiment, let’s perform a t-test to compare the means of the control and treatment groups.

Python Code:

from scipy.stats import ttest_ind



# Perform t-test

control = experiment_data[experiment_data['Group'] == 'Control']['Outcome']

treatment = experiment_data[experiment_data['Group'] == 'Treatment']['Outcome']

t_stat, p_value = ttest_ind(control, treatment)



print(f"T-statistic: {t_stat}, P-value: {p_value}")

This code performs a t-test to compare the means of the control and treatment groups. A low p-value indicates a significant difference between the groups.

What does a low p-value (< 0.05) in a t-test indicate?

  • A) No significant difference between groups

  • B) A significant difference between groups

  • C) A larger sample size is needed

  • D) The experiment was poorly designed

The correct answer is B) A significant difference between groups. A low p-value suggests that the observed effect is statistically significan
